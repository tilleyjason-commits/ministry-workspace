# Walk in Love — Ephesians 5:1–2

**Series:** Ephesians: Our Riches in Christ
**Text:** Ephesians 5:1–2
**Length:** ~20 minutes

---

## Introduction

In 1878, British Prime Minister William Gladstone stood before Parliament to announce the death of Princess Alice, Queen Victoria's daughter. But it wasn't just a formal announcement — he told a story.

Princess Alice's youngest child, Marie, had died of diphtheria during an epidemic. The grieving mother concealed the news from her other children to protect them. But when her son Ernest — who was especially devoted to his little sister — learned what had happened, he broke down in uncontrollable grief.

Alice, desperate to comfort her weeping son, did something the doctor had warned her never to do. She clasped him in her arms and kissed him — a kiss of pure, selfless love. Within a week, she had caught the infection and was dead.

That mother measured love not by what it gained, but by what it gave. And what a picture that gives us this morning — because that's exactly the kind of love our Heavenly Father has shown us.

Turn with me to **Ephesians 5:1–2**.

We've spent weeks walking through chapters 1 through 3, marveling at the staggering truth of what God has done for us. Paul has been building a doctrinal mountain:
- **Chosen** before the foundation of the world (1:4)
- **Adopted** as sons and daughters through Jesus Christ (1:5)
- **Sealed** with the Holy Spirit of promise (1:13)
- **Saved** by grace through faith — not of works (2:8–9)
- **Made** fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God (2:19)

We've been sitting at the feet of God's love — soaking in the reality that the Father loves us the same way He loves His own Son (John 17:23). But now Paul turns a corner. And if you've been paying attention to the depth of God's love, what Paul says next shouldn't feel like a burden. It should feel like the most natural thing in the world.

Let's read it together:

> *"Therefore, be imitators of God, as beloved children. And walk in love, as Christ also loved us and gave Himself for us, a sacrificial and fragrant offering to God."*

---

## Point 1: More Than a Name — The Call to Be Genuine

**Ephesians 5:1 — "Be imitators of God, as beloved children."**

There's a difference between being *called* a Christian and being *a* Christian in the way you actually live. Paul's not talking about a label. He's talking about a life.

The word "followers" here — *mimētai* — literally means "imitators." It's where we get our word "mimic." Paul is saying: mimic God. Copy Him. Let your life look like His life.

Now, you might be thinking — that sounds impossible. God is perfect. I'm flawed. He's holy. I struggle. How can I possibly be an imitator of God?

And you'd be right — **if it were a command you had to accomplish in your own strength.**

But look at the beautiful condition Paul gives: **"as beloved children."**

You know what children do naturally? They imitate their parents. A little boy walks around the house with his daddy's tool belt dragging on the floor. A little girl puts on her mother's shoes and clomps around the kitchen. Why? Because she's a daughter. She wants to be like the one she loves.

Paul says: That's you. You're not a slave trying to earn your place. You're a **beloved child**. The word *tekna* means "born ones" — those who share the very nature of the Father. The love has already been poured into your heart by the Holy Spirit (Romans 5:5). This isn't about trying harder — it's about *being who you already are*.

**Illustration:** Think about a child who's adopted into a loving family. Before adoption, that child is a stranger — trying to figure out how to behave, uncertain of their place. But after the adoption papers are signed, everything changes. They have a new name. A new identity. A new inheritance. And over time, if that home is filled with love, the child begins to reflect the parents — their mannerisms, their values, their way of speaking. Not because they're forced to. Because they're *loved* into it.

That's exactly what Paul has been telling us in Ephesians 1. We've been **adopted** as sons and daughters. Not because we earned it, but because the Father chose us in love.

This is the difference between being a Christian in name only and being a Christian from the heart. A person who merely wears the label lives by a list of rules — do this, don't do that — and it becomes drudgery. But a person who knows they are a *beloved child* — who has tasted the Father's love — finds that love becomes the engine of their obedience.

**Application this week:** Ask yourself: *Am I obeying God because I feel obligated, or because I'm responding to His love?* If your Christian walk feels like a duty checklist, you may have forgotten that you're not a hired hand — you're a son or a daughter. Take time this week to sit with the truth that the Father loves you the same way He loves Jesus. Let that sink in before you try to do anything *for* Him.

---

## Point 2: Love, Not Law — The Motivation That Changes Everything

**Ephesians 5:2a — "And walk in love..."**

Paul doesn't say: "Now here are twenty new rules to follow." He says: **Walk in love.**

The word "walk" means "to regulate your entire life." Let every step, every decision, every word be governed by love. And the kind of love Paul is talking about — *agapē* — is the unconditional, undeserved, self-sacrificing love of God Himself.

You see, the whole Christian life hangs on this one thing. Jesus said it plainly in Matthew 22:

> *"You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your mind. This is the first and great commandment. And the second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself."*

Everything else hangs on these. Not the other way around.

And here's where we so easily get it backwards. We tend to reduce the Christian life to a list:
- Don't lie.
- Don't steal.
- Don't be angry.
- Don't use foul language.
- Go to church.
- Read your Bible.
- Pray.

And before you know it, you're living by a code instead of by a relationship. You're checking boxes instead of walking in love.

But Paul isn't giving you a rulebook. He's pointing you to a **Person**. And that Person — Jesus — didn't live by a list. He **walked in love**. Everything He did flowed from His love for the Father and His love for people.

**Illustration:** Let me tell you about a grandmother in a story by J.F. Powers called "The Trouble."

During a terrible race riot in a major American city, a black family watches the chaos from their apartment window. Whites chasing blacks. Blacks chasing whites. It's absolute bedlam.

And then a young boy at the window sees a white man running down the street. He turns into an alley behind the building — but it's a dead end. A gang of black youths comes around the corner after him.

The boy recoils in horror, knowing what's about to happen.

But just then, the back door of the apartment building swings open. It's the boy's grandmother. She saw the man's plight. She left the safety of her window and went down to help him.

The boy's mind swirls: *I was glad for the white man until I remembered Mama all broken to pieces on the bed. And then I was sorry Grandma did it. And then again, I was happy she did. Then I got ahold of this funny idea: the trouble is, somebody gets cheated, or insulted, or killed. And everybody thinks they can make it come out even by cheating, insulting, and killing the cheaters, the insulters, and the killers. Only they never do.*

That boy understood something profound. The cycle of revenge never ends. Rules and retaliation can't break it.

The only thing that breaks the cycle is love — the kind of love that risks everything for someone who doesn't deserve it.

That grandmother didn't consult a rulebook. She didn't stop to think, "Is this theologically correct?" She saw a person in need, and love moved her to act.

That's the kind of walk Paul is talking about. A life where love — not a list — is your compass.

**Application this week:** When you face a decision this week — especially a difficult one involving another person — don't ask yourself first, "What's the rule here?" Ask yourself: *What does love look like in this moment?* If you're tempted to respond with harsh words, ask: *How would love speak?* If you're tempted to hold a grudge, ask: *How would love release?* Love isn't a feeling you have to manufacture — it's the nature of the Father living in you. Let it guide your feet.

---

## Point 3: The Measure of Love — What It Gives, Not What It Gains

**Ephesians 5:2b — "...as Christ also loved us and gave Himself for us, a sacrificial and fragrant offering to God."**

Now Paul shows us the ultimate model. He doesn't just say "love." He shows us what love looks like.

**"Christ also loved us and gave Himself for us."**

This is the heart of the gospel. Jesus didn't just feel affection for us from a distance. He didn't send a card. He didn't say a prayer and hope things worked out. He **gave Himself** — laid down His life — as an offering and a sacrifice to God on our behalf.

Paul reaches back to the Old Testament sacrificial system to explain this. He uses two words:

- **"Offering"** *(prosphora)* — this pictures the grain offering, the meal offering. It speaks of Christ's perfect character and total devotion to the Father.
- **"Sacrifice"** *(thysia)* — this pictures the peace offering, where the animal was slain. It speaks of Christ giving His life to make peace between sinners and God.

And Paul says this whole work was **"a fragrant offering to God."** In other words, the Father looked at what Jesus did on the cross and said, "This pleases Me completely."

Now here's the amazing thing: **the same love that moved Jesus to the cross is now living in you.** And the way we show that love to others is the same way Jesus showed it to us — by giving ourselves away.

**Illustration:** There's a story about a little boy whose mother baked a fresh batch of cookies and put them in a cookie jar with strict instructions: no one touch them until after dinner. It wasn't long before she heard the lid rattle, and she called out, "My son, what are you doing?" A meek voice called back: "My hand is in the cookie jar resisting temptation!"

That's funny, but it's also a picture of how many of us try to live the Christian life. We put ourselves in the middle of temptation and think we can resist by sheer willpower. We try to white-knuckle our way through obedience.

But the Christian life doesn't work that way. You can't resist the world's pull by trying harder to follow a list of prohibitions. The only way to truly overcome the pull of sin is to be so captivated by a greater love that the lesser things lose their power.

When you truly understand that Christ gave Himself **for you** — when you grasp that you are a **beloved child** of the Most High God — suddenly the cheap imitations the world offers don't look so appealing anymore.

A woman who knows she's deeply loved by her husband doesn't need a rulebook to tell her not to flirt with other men. Her love for her husband naturally steers her heart. In the same way, when we know we are loved by Christ with an everlasting love, obedience becomes the natural response of a grateful heart — not a burdensome duty.

**Application this week:** Take five minutes each day this week to reflect on the cross. Not in a religious, going-through-the-motions way, but really pause and ask: *Jesus gave Himself for me. What am I holding back from Him?* Is there a relationship you need to repair? A grudge you need to release? A habit you need to surrender? Is there someone in your life right now who needs you to give of yourself for their good? Ask God to show you one practical way this week you can *give yourself* for someone else — not because you have to, but because His love compels you.

---

## Conclusion

As we close, I want you to hear this clearly: **Genuine love is always measured by what it gives, not by what it gains.**

The world tells you to measure love by what you get out of it. But God's love — the love that made you His child — measures love by what it sacrifices.

- That's the kind of love that moved Princess Alice to kiss her son, knowing it could cost her everything.
- That's the kind of love that sent a grandmother into a deadly riot to save a stranger.
- That's the kind of love that **sent Jesus to the cross while we were still sinners, enemies, and undeserving.**

You are not called to be a Christian in name only. You are called to be **an imitator of God** — walking in love as His beloved child.

Not because you have to. Not because there's a rulebook you're afraid to break. But because the love of Christ constrains you, compels you, and carries you.

The old nature builds walls and declares war. But the new nature — the nature you received when you were born again into God's family — builds bridges and proclaims peace.

So I'll leave you with this question from the text itself:

**Can others see the Father in you?**

Not your church attendance. Not your Bible knowledge. Not your religious vocabulary. Can they see His love in the way you treat people — especially the ones who don't deserve it?

If you've been living by a list, it's time to start living by love. If you've been serving out of duty, it's time to rediscover the delight of being a child of God. And if you've never truly understood what it means to be adopted into His family — if you're still standing outside, trying to earn what only grace can give — then today is the day to come home.

The Father's arms are open. His love is waiting.

*Let's pray.*

> Father, thank You for making us Your beloved children. Thank You that we don't have to earn Your love — we already have it fully in Christ. Lord, forgive us for the times we've reduced the Christian life to a list of rules and missed the heart of it all. By Your Spirit, teach us to walk in love — the way Jesus walked. Let our lives be a fragrant offering to You, not because we're trying to impress You, but because we're responding to the greatest love the world has ever known. In Jesus' name, Amen.
