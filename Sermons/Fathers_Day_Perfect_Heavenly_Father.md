# The Perfect Heavenly Father

**Occasion:** Father's Day  
**Length:** Approximately 15 minutes  
**Primary Texts:** Matthew 7:9-11; John 14:8-9; Hebrews 1:1-3  
**Big Idea:** God uses the image of an earthly father to help us understand His heart, and Jesus Christ shows us the Father perfectly.

## Sermon Manuscript

Happy Father's Day.

Today is a day when many people are grateful. Some are thinking about a father who loved them, prayed for them, corrected them, worked hard for them, and showed up when it mattered. Others may feel the weight of this day differently. Maybe your father was absent. Maybe he was harsh. Maybe he tried, but he was broken. Maybe you are a father and you feel the gap between the man you want to be and the man you have been.

So when the Bible calls God "Father," that word can land in different places in our hearts.

But here is the good news: God does not call Himself Father because He is trying to copy the best of earthly fathers. Earthly fathers, at their best, are only a faint picture of Him. God is not the enlarged version of our earthly experience. He is the perfect original. He is the Father from whom every good fatherhood gets its meaning.

Jesus said in Matthew 7:9-11:

> "Or what man is there of you, whom if his son ask bread, will he give him a stone? Or if he ask a fish, will he give him a serpent? If ye then, being evil, know how to give good gifts unto your children, how much more shall your Father which is in heaven give good things to them that ask him?"

Jesus is saying, "Look at the best instincts of a father. Even flawed people know something about giving, protecting, and caring for their children. Now take that picture and understand this: your heavenly Father is infinitely better."

So today I want to preach on this thought: **The Perfect Heavenly Father.**

God uses the imagery of an earthly father to show us who He is, and then He shows us His heart most clearly in Jesus Christ.

## 1. A Father Gives Life

The first thing a father represents is life. A child does not create himself. A child receives life.

That is true naturally, and it is true spiritually. God is our Creator. Acts 17:28 says, "For in him we live, and move, and have our being." Every breath we take is a gift from the Father.

But Scripture goes even deeper. God is not only the Creator of physical life. He is the giver of new life.

John 1:12-13 says:

> "But as many as received him, to them gave he power to become the sons of God, even to them that believe on his name: which were born, not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God."

A good earthly father may give a child his name, his home, his provision, and his care. But only God can give a person a new heart.

And where do we see this most clearly? In Jesus Christ.

Jesus told Nicodemus, "Except a man be born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God." Then Jesus went to the cross to make that new birth possible. Through His death, burial, and resurrection, sinners can be made sons and daughters of God.

The Father gives life, and He gives that life through the Son.

1 John 5:12 says, "He that hath the Son hath life; and he that hath not the Son of God hath not life."

So before we talk about what kind of father we should be, or what kind of children we should be, we must start here: Have you received life from the Father through faith in Jesus Christ?

Because God is not merely offering advice. He is offering adoption. He is not simply trying to improve your old life. He is giving you new life in His Son.

## 2. A Father Provides

When we think of a father, we often think of provision. Not just money, but care. Food on the table. Clothes to wear. A roof overhead. Help when there is a need.

Jesus uses that exact image in Matthew 7. If a child asks for bread, a decent father does not hand him a stone. If he asks for fish, he does not hand him a serpent. A father does not mock the hunger of his child. He provides.

Then Jesus says, "How much more shall your Father which is in heaven give good things to them that ask him?"

That phrase "how much more" matters. Jesus is teaching us not to measure God downward by human failure, but upward from human goodness.

If an imperfect father can still know how to give good gifts, how much more can the perfect Father provide what His children need?

Philippians 4:19 says, "But my God shall supply all your need according to his riches in glory by Christ Jesus."

Notice that phrase: "by Christ Jesus."

God's greatest provision is not merely daily bread. His greatest provision is the Bread of Life.

Jesus said in John 6:35, "I am the bread of life: he that cometh to me shall never hunger; and he that believeth on me shall never thirst."

The Father saw our deepest need. We needed more than better circumstances. We needed forgiveness. We needed reconciliation. We needed righteousness. We needed a Savior.

So what did the Father provide?

He provided His Son.

Romans 8:32 says:

> "He that spared not his own Son, but delivered him up for us all, how shall he not with him also freely give us all things?"

That means the cross is the final proof that the Father is not stingy, distant, or unwilling. The Father gave what was most precious to meet our greatest need.

If you ever wonder whether God cares, look at Jesus.

If you ever wonder whether God will provide, look at Jesus.

If you ever wonder whether God is holding back His heart from you, look at Jesus on the cross.

The Father provides, and His greatest provision is Christ.

## 3. A Father Protects

A father is also meant to protect. A good father does not expose his children to danger casually. He watches. He guards. He steps in. He stands between the child and what would destroy them.

Psalm 46:1 says, "God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble."

Psalm 91:2 says, "I will say of the LORD, He is my refuge and my fortress: my God; in him will I trust."

Now we must be careful. God's protection does not mean His children never face hardship. Many faithful believers have suffered deeply. Jesus Himself said, "In the world ye shall have tribulation."

But the Father protects us in ways deeper than comfort. He protects our souls. He keeps His promises. He holds His children. He does not abandon them to sin, death, or the enemy.

And again, we see this in Jesus.

Jesus said in John 10:11, "I am the good shepherd: the good shepherd giveth his life for the sheep."

That is fatherly protection. The Son stands in the place of danger. He lays down His life so the sheep may live.

At the cross, Jesus did not protect Himself from suffering. He protected us through His suffering. He took the judgment we deserved. He bore the wrath we could not bear. He defeated the power of sin and death.

Then He rose again, and He says in John 10:28:

> "And I give unto them eternal life; and they shall never perish, neither shall any man pluck them out of my hand."

That is security.

The Father's children are not held by their own strength. They are held by the hand of Christ.

Some of us live like spiritual orphans, always afraid that one bad day, one weak moment, one unanswered prayer means we have been abandoned. But the gospel says the Father has not left you unprotected. He has placed you in Christ.

The Father protects, and He protects us through the saving work and keeping power of Jesus.

## 4. A Father Corrects

This may not be the part of fatherhood we like to talk about on Father's Day, but it is one of the most loving parts.

A good father corrects his children.

Not because he enjoys control. Not because he wants to crush them. But because he loves them too much to let them destroy themselves.

Hebrews 12:6 says, "For whom the Lord loveth he chasteneth, and scourgeth every son whom he receiveth."

The world often treats correction as rejection. But Scripture says godly correction is proof of relationship.

If a child is running toward danger, love does not say, "I do not want to interfere." Love calls out. Love reaches out. Love corrects.

God's correction is not the anger of an unstable father. It is the discipline of a holy Father. It is purposeful. It is wise. It is aimed at our good.

Hebrews 12:10 says He disciplines us "for our profit, that we might be partakers of his holiness."

And where do we see the Father's holiness and correction most clearly? In Jesus Christ.

Jesus is full of grace and truth. He receives sinners, but He does not leave them in sin.

To the woman caught in adultery, He said, "Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more." That is grace and correction together.

To Peter, after Peter denied Him three times, Jesus did not throw him away. He restored him. But He also asked him, "Lovest thou me?" He brought Peter's heart back to the center.

Jesus shows us that the Father's correction is redemptive. It is not meant to humiliate us. It is meant to heal us.

So when the Word of God corrects you, do not harden your heart. When the Spirit convicts you, do not run from the Father. Run to Him.

The same Father who corrects is the Father who restores.

## 5. A Father Is Present

One of the deepest needs of a child is presence. A father may give gifts, but if he is never there, the child still feels the absence.

Presence says, "You matter. I see you. I am with you."

The Bible reveals God as a present Father.

Psalm 34:18 says, "The LORD is nigh unto them that are of a broken heart."

Isaiah 41:10 says, "Fear thou not; for I am with thee."

But the ultimate proof of God's presence is Jesus Christ.

Matthew 1:23 says His name shall be called Emmanuel, "God with us."

That is astonishing. The Father did not love us from a distance only. He sent His Son into our world. Jesus stepped into our weakness, our grief, our temptation, our suffering, and our death.

When Philip said, "Lord, shew us the Father," Jesus answered in John 14:9:

> "Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father."

If you want to know what the Father is like, look at Jesus touching lepers.

Look at Jesus blessing children.

Look at Jesus weeping at Lazarus' tomb.

Look at Jesus feeding the hungry.

Look at Jesus forgiving sinners.

Look at Jesus praying for His disciples.

Look at Jesus hanging on the cross, saying, "Father, forgive them."

Jesus is not a contradiction of the Father. Jesus is the perfect revelation of the Father.

Hebrews 1:3 says Jesus is "the brightness of his glory, and the express image of his person."

So we do not have to guess what God is like. We have seen Him in the Son.

The Father is present, and He has come near to us in Jesus Christ.

## 6. A Father Gives Identity

A father also gives identity. In many cultures, a child receives the father's name. That name means belonging. It says, "You are part of this family."

One of the most beautiful truths in Scripture is that believers are not merely forgiven criminals. We are adopted children.

Romans 8:15 says:

> "For ye have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear; but ye have received the Spirit of adoption, whereby we cry, Abba, Father."

That word "Abba" carries closeness and trust. It is the cry of a child who knows they belong.

And that belonging comes through Jesus.

Ephesians 1:5 says God has predestined us "unto the adoption of children by Jesus Christ to himself, according to the good pleasure of his will."

Jesus, the eternal Son of God, brings us into the family of God. By nature, He is the Son. By grace, we become sons and daughters in Him.

That changes how we live.

You do not have to live for the approval of people when you are accepted in the Beloved.

You do not have to be defined by your past when the Father has given you a new name.

You do not have to live like an orphan when the Spirit teaches your heart to cry, "Abba, Father."

For some, Father's Day reminds you of wounds. The gospel does not pretend those wounds are small. But it does declare that your deepest identity does not have to be defined by who left, who failed, or who hurt you. In Christ, you can know the perfect Father.

And for fathers, this is both humbling and hopeful. You are not called to be the perfect father. Only God is that. But you are called to point beyond yourself to Him.

When you provide, you give a small picture of the Father's care.

When you protect, you give a small picture of the Father's refuge.

When you correct with love, you give a small picture of the Father's holiness.

When you are present, you give a small picture of Emmanuel, God with us.

When you forgive, you give a small picture of the gospel.

Your children do not need you to pretend to be perfect. They need you to walk humbly with the perfect Father.

## Conclusion: Come Home to the Father

Jesus told a story in Luke 15 about a son who left home. He took his inheritance, wasted it, and ended up broken in a far country.

But when he came to himself, he said, "I will arise and go to my father."

And while he was still a great way off, his father saw him, had compassion, ran, fell on his neck, and kissed him.

That is the heart Jesus wants us to see.

God is not a reluctant Father waiting for you to prove yourself worthy. He is a merciful Father who receives repentant sinners through His Son.

And the reason He can receive us is because Jesus went to the cross. The beloved Son took our place, bore our sin, and rose again so that prodigals could come home.

So today, if you are a father, look to the heavenly Father and let Him teach you how to love.

If you are grateful for a godly father, give thanks, and remember that every good thing you saw in him was a reflection of a greater Father.

If this day is painful, bring that pain to the Father who is near to the brokenhearted.

If you have been living far from God, come home.

The Father gives life.  
The Father provides.  
The Father protects.  
The Father corrects.  
The Father is present.  
The Father gives identity.  

And we see Him perfectly in Jesus Christ.

So let us fix our eyes on the Son and trust the heart of the Father.

Amen.
