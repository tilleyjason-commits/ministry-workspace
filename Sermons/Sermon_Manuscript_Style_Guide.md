## 0. How to Use This Guide

This document is the **sole master specification** for sermon manuscripts. When a new sermon is requested, the manuscript must match **everything** described here unless the pastor explicitly overrides a section:

- the **theological posture** (Section 1)
- the **voice and tone** (Section 2) — with concrete examples from the sample

## 1. Theological Posture (Non-Negotiable Foundation)

Every manuscript is written from a **Reformed Baptist** framework, holding to the **1689 London Baptist Confession**.

**Core commitments that shape the writing:**

1. **Grace precedes command.** This is the spine of the entire sermon. Indicatives (who we are in Christ) always come *before* imperatives (what we do). The sermon must make the listener feel that obedience flows *out of* an identity already given, never that identity is *earned by* obedience. In "More Than a Name," this is the literal thesis — "you act like God *because* you've been adopted, not in order to be adopted."

2. **Christ-centered.** Wherever the text supports it, the sermon drives to Christ and to the cross. The third point of "More Than a Name" deliberately grounds the command to love in Christ's self-giving *so that the command never collapses into "a higher rule."* This move — refusing to leave a command standing on its own without the gospel underneath it — should recur in every manuscript.

3. **Faithful exposition.** The sermon stays close to the text. The central idea of the sermon must be the central idea of the *passage*. Do not import a topic and decorate it with verses; draw the message out of what the text actually says.

4. **The tone of the text governs the tone of the sermon.** Warning passages must sound weighty. Comfort passages must sound comforting. Never flatten the emotional register of Scripture. (E.g., the cross section in Section III is meant to feel weighty and tender, not informational.)

5. **Cessationist** on apostles and prophets (Eph. 2:20). No continuationist assumptions.

6. **Scripture is the supreme authority.** Outside quotations and illustrations *serve* the text; they never stand beside it as equal authorities. Secular sources are subordinated, always.

7. **Doctrinal precision.** No proof-text misapplication. No folk etymology. Word studies must be linguistically legitimate (e.g., real Greek/Hebrew, not invented English derivations).

## 2. Voice and Tone

This is the heart of the format. The manuscript should read as if a **warm, plain-spoken pastor who loves his people** is talking *to* them — not lecturing, not performing, not reading an essay.

### 2.1 Overall character of the voice
- **Pastorally warm but spiritually serious.** Affectionate toward the congregation, honest about sin, never harsh, never flippant.
- **Conversational, not academic.** The manuscript is written to be *spoken aloud*. It should sound natural coming out of the mouth. Read every paragraph as if preaching it.
- **Direct address.** Speak to "you" and "we." Pull the listener in. Ask them real questions ("Is it possible to be a Christian in name — and not be a Christian in the way we actually live?").
- **Unhurried confidence.** Short declarative sentences carry weight. The voice is calm and sure, not frantic or hype-driven.
- **Tender at the gospel.** When the sermon reaches Christ and the cross, the language softens and slows. Words like *melt us*, *staggering*, *tender* signal the shift. Let the comfort be comforting.

### 2.2 Devices the voice uses — with concrete examples from "More Than a Name"

| Device | How It Works | Example from Sample |
|---|---|---|
| **Rhetorical questions that press conscience** | Asks a question the listener can't easily dodge — personal, searching, not rhetorical in the dismissive sense | *"Is it possible to be a Christian in name — and not be a Christian in the way we actually live?"* |
| **The honest admission** | Preacher stands *with* the congregation under the Word, not above it. Names the temptation we all share. | *"And this is where I have to be honest about a temptation every serious believer faces. We can take the commands of God and turn them into a checklist."* |
| **The vivid, ordinary picture** | Abstract truth made concrete through everyday images the listener has seen | *"The boy stands at the mirror with a razor and no whiskers, dragging it across his face the way he watched Dad do it."* |
| | | *"A hired hand watches the clock. A son works the family farm because it's his family's farm. Same chores — completely different heart."* |
| **The repeated key word** | A load-bearing word repeated deliberately to drive it deep | *beloved* (used throughout Point I, never letting the identity fade); *agape* (explained once, then the concept carries the rest of the sermon); *gave* (hammered in Point III) |
| **The turn** | A sentence that pivots from comfort to challenge or challenge to comfort, often beginning with "But," "And yet," "Now," or "So" | *"Now if you stop and think about that command, it should stagger you..." → "But he doesn't stop there..."* |
| **Contrast pairs** | Truth sharpened by its opposite | obligation vs. love; checklist vs. captured by a love; name on the roll vs. features on the face; hired hand vs. son; clock-watcher vs. family farmer |
| **Short punch after long** | A one-sentence landing after a rolling paragraph — lands like a hammer | *"Likeness flows from belonging."* |
| | | *"It is a decision before it is ever a feeling."* |
| **The crescendo triplet** | Three short phrases building rhythm at a climax | *"It heals homes. It holds churches together. It melts the hearts of people who think they're beyond reach."* |
| **The "therefore" move** | Pauses on a small but crucial word to unpack its weight | *"Notice that little word 'therefore.' It reaches back and grabs everything that came before it."* |
| **The "and here is what should melt us" moment** | Explicit signal to the congregation: this is the tender part, lean in | *"And here is what should melt us: the Father speaks of you the way He spoke of His own Son."* |
| **The takeaway question** | A short, memorable question the listener can carry out the door and ask themselves all week | *"Who can I love today the way my Father has loved me?"* |
| **The decisive question** | The one question everything comes down to — lands just before the invitation | *"So the question that decides all of it is this: Are you His child?"* |

### 2.3 What the voice avoids
- No academic throat-clearing, no jargon dumped on the congregation untranslated. Greek/Hebrew is *explained in plain words* the moment it appears.
- No cynicism, sarcasm, or cleverness for its own sake.
- No manipulation, guilt-driving, or emotional pressure. Invitations are warm, never coercive.
- No filler. Every sentence earns its place.
- No flattering the congregation and no talking down to them.

### 2.4 Tone calibration by section

| Section | Tone | Sample Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | Engaging, honest, slightly unsettling — raises the real question | Opens with a fallen Bible professor — the question lands quietly, not shouted |
| Point I | Warm, reassuring — establishes identity and belonging | *"Beloved children"* repeated; the boy-with-razor image is tender, funny, and warm |
| Point II | Convicting but kind — names the checklist temptation, calls higher | *"This is where I have to be honest..."* — the preacher includes himself |
| Point III | Weighty and tender — the cross; slows down, deepens | *"Go to Calvary and look at the Lamb of God dying for sinners. There is the definition of the word."* |
| Conclusion | Searching then hopeful — presses the question, then opens the door | Bookends to the professor → *"Are you His child?"* → two-pronged invitation |
| Closing lines | Lifted, memorable, worshipful | Centered, bold italic, navy — short, poetic, unforgettable |

## 3. Structural Skeleton

The manuscript follows a fixed architecture. **Three main points** is the default and strongly preferred when the text allows.

### 3.1 Required components (in order)

1. **Title block** — title, subtitle, passage + (ESV), church/byline. (All centered, see Section 8.)
2. **Big Idea** — one or two sentences stating the central thesis (boxed; see Section 8 for formatting). This is the sermon in a sentence and everything serves it.
3. **Introduction** — follows this exact internal sequence:
   - **Opening illustration** — a story that hooks the listener and raises the central tension
   - **Central question** — the uncomfortable question the illustration forces on us
   - **Context bridge** — "Paul has spent X chapters telling the Ephesians who they are..." (a rapid summary of what's been built so far in the series/letter)
   - **"Listen to the text"** — the spoken cue that the Scripture is coming
   - **Anchor Scripture** — the passage read in full, indented, italic, with bold navy citation
   - **The "therefore" takeaway** — unpacking the connective word that ties the passage to what came before
   - **Three points announced** — "This morning I want us to see three things in these two verses—[Point I], [Point II], and [Point III]."
4. **Point I** — exposition + **Application** subheading.
5. **Point II** — exposition + **Application** subheading.
6. **Point III** — exposition + **Application** subheading.
7. **Conclusion** — follows this exact internal sequence:
   - **Bookend** — reaches back to the opening illustration: "So let me bring it back to where we started."
   - **Restate the central question** with fresh urgency
   - **What's at stake** — one paragraph on why this matters
   - **The decisive question** — "So the question that decides all of it is this: Are you His child?"
8. **Response / Invitation** — a short, numbered, two-pronged appeal:
   - **Item 1** ("If you are — then go and live like it...") — to those already in Christ
   - **Item 2** ("If you are not — then hear the better news first...") — to those not yet in Christ
9. **Closing lines** — one or two centered, bold-italic lines that crystallize the message memorably.

### 3.2 Point construction — the internal rhythm

Each main point follows a consistent movement. This rhythm is not optional:

```
State the text
  → Acknowledge how it sounds to fallen ears ("Now if you stop and think about that command, it should stagger you...")
  → Expand the word or phrase that carries the weight (word study, Greek/Hebrew)
  → Show what the text does NOT mean (guardrail against misunderstanding)
  → The turn to what it DOES mean ("But he doesn't stop there...")
  → Illustrate it (one strong illustration)
  → Connect the illustration immediately back to the text ("That is the new way. That is what it looks like to...")
  → Tie in cross-references naturally
  → [Application subheading]
  → Concrete, this-week, pastoral application
  → Takeaway question the listener can carry out the door
  → Grace-anchor: "you are not left to pull this off alone..."
```

**Point III always has an additional move:** after the exposition, it must explicitly *ground the command in the gospel* so the sermon never collapses into moralism. The move is: *"Now, if I tell you to ______ and stop there, I've only given you another rule — a higher, harder rule. So Paul doesn't stop. He shows us what this looks like, and he points to the cross."* Then proceed to Christ's work as the foundation.

### 3.3 The bookend principle

The **opening illustration and the conclusion are tied together.** Whatever real-life situation or story opens the sermon should return at the close so the message lands as a single, unified arc. In "More Than a Name," the fallen Bible professor opens the question and closes it — the sermon returns to that image just before the invitation, showing that the stakes are real.

### 3.4 Length discipline
- Default target: **~30 minutes** unless another length is requested.
- "More Than a Name" was a **~20-minute** sermon; honor whatever length the pastor specifies and scale the manuscript accordingly. Roughly: 20 min ≈ tighter exposition, one illustration per point; 30 min ≈ fuller development with more cross-references and slightly broader illustration.
- Material that belongs to a *later* passage in a series is held back, not forced in early (e.g., the cookie-jar illustration was reserved for Eph. 5:3–7 rather than crammed into 5:1–2). Respect the series arc.

### 3.5 Point header alliteration

Alliterated or parallel point headings are **valued** when they arise naturally from the text. The sample demonstrates this:
- **I. There Is a Person to Imitate (v. 1)**
- **II. There Is a Pattern to Live Out (v. 2a)**
- **III. There Is a Proof That Holds It Up (v. 2b)**

Never force alliteration at the cost of accuracy. If natural parallels don't emerge, straightforward headers are fine.

## 4. Illustrations

Illustrations are essential and frequent, but disciplined.

### 4.1 Rules
- **One strong illustration per main point** is the working rhythm (more for a 30-min sermon if they earn their place).
- **An opening illustration and a closing illustration are required** (the closing one is always the opening one returning — the bookend principle).
- Illustrations **serve the point**, never decorate it. After every illustration, the manuscript immediately connects it back to the text and the truth. From the sample: *"That is the new way. That is what it looks like to 'walk in love' when love costs you something and the other person hasn't earned it. That grandmother imitated the Father..."*
- **Ordinary-life pictures** are preferred and powerful: the child mimicking a parent, the hired hand vs. the son, the clock-watcher. These require no sourcing and connect instantly.
- **Honest framing of borrowed stories.** If a story is fiction, a composite, or an allegory, it must be framed honestly in the pulpit — never presented as a true event if it isn't. The J.F. Powers material is fiction and is introduced as *"a moving scene in a short story,"* not as something that happened.
- **The opening illustration must carry weight.** It should not be a throwaway joke or a mere attention-getter. It should raise a genuine, uncomfortable question that the sermon will answer. The Bible professor story in the sample does exactly this — it makes the listener feel the tension immediately.
- **Secular quotes and sources stay subordinate** to Scripture and are used sparingly.

### 4.2 Attribution discipline
- Any quotation attributed to a real person must be **verified** before use. Unverified or shaky attributions are flagged or dropped, never guessed.
- Reformed/Puritan sources (Spurgeon, Tozer, Lloyd-Jones, Wiersbe, Moody, etc.) are welcome when the attribution is confirmed.

### 4.3 The illustration funnel — how each illustration is deployed

```
Tell the story (concise, vivid, sensory)
  → Pause to let it land
  → Explicit connective: "That is..."
  → Tie it directly to the text: "That is what it looks like to..."
  → Reinforce with a cross-reference: "As Paul says in..."
```

From the sample (Point II, the grandmother illustration):
1. *"There's a moving scene in a short story set during a riot in an American city..."* (tell the story — ~6 sentences)
2. *"The boy wrestles with it: the cycle of getting even never actually ends."* (let it land — reflective pause)
3. *"That is the new way. That is what it looks like to 'walk in love' when love costs you something and the other person hasn't earned it."* (explicit connective)
4. *"That grandmother imitated the Father, who 'shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us' (Romans 5:8)."* (cross-reference anchor)

This exact rhythm should be followed for every illustration.

## 5. Scripture Handling

- **ESV throughout**, always, unless the pastor requests another translation. If a source quotes another translation (KJV, RSV, NEB, etc.), **silently convert it to ESV** in the manuscript.
- **One anchor text** (the preaching passage) is read in full early in the introduction, set off and cited. Always preceded by the spoken cue: *"Listen to the text."*
- **Supporting cross-references** are woven through naturally to substantiate each point. They are cited inline in parentheses, e.g., "(Ephesians 1:5)," "(Romans 5:8)."
- Cross-references should be **abundant but purposeful** — each one actually supports the claim it follows. No proof-text padding.
- Scripture quotations are formatted in **italics**; the citation that follows is **not** italicized (and in the manuscript is often bold/navy — see Section 8 for exact formatting).
- Word studies (Greek/Hebrew) must be **legitimate and immediately explained in plain English**. From the sample: *"the word translated imitators is the word we get 'mimic' from"* — two seconds to understand, no jargon.

### 5.1 Cross-reference density
Aim for roughly 2–3 cross-references per point (beyond the anchor text). They should feel woven in naturally, not stacked like proof-texts. From the sample:
- Point I: Ephesians 1:5, 1 John 3:1, Matthew 3:17, John 17:23, John 14:9, Philippians 2:13
- Point II: 1 John 4:8, Matthew 22:37–40, Romans 5:8, Romans 5:10
- Point III: Philippians 2:5–8, 1 Peter 2:24, 2 Corinthians 5:21, John 15:13

Each cross-reference is introduced conversationally, not announced: *"Listen to what Paul has already said..."* / *"As John marvels..."* / *"And watch how far down that love reached..."*

## 6. Application

- Each main point ends with an explicit **Application** subheading (formatted per Section 8.7).
- Application is **concrete, practical, and pastoral** — what this looks like in the listener's actual week. From the sample:
  - Point I: *"When people who live with you watch you closely — your spouse, your kids, the people in the pew next to you — do they catch a glimpse of the Father?"*
  - Point II: *"It's forgiving the family member who never said sorry. It's the kind word to the person who can do nothing for you. It's breaking the cycle when everything in you wants to get even."*
  - Point III: *"When you forgive someone who can't repay you, when you serve where no one notices, when you love the person who can never love you back the same way — the Bible says that kind of love rises to God like a fragrant offering."*
- Application **reinforces the grace-before-command spine**: it tells people to live *from* their identity, and reminds them they are not doing it in their own strength. Each Application in the sample closes with a grace-anchor: *"And hear this so you don't walk out crushed: you are not left to pull this off alone. 'It is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure' (Philippians 2:13)."*
- Application asks a **takeaway question** the listener can carry out the door: *"Who can I love today the way my Father has loved me?"*
- Never moralistic, never a bare to-do list. Always tethered to the gospel just preached.

### 6.1 The Application rhythm

Each Application block follows this internal sequence:

```
[Application subheading]
→ Press the point home with a personal question
→ Make it concrete with 2–3 specific examples of what this looks like
→ Offer the takeaway question
→ Anchor in grace: "you are not left to pull this off alone..."
```

## 7. Sentence-Level Writing Mechanics

These are the fine-grained habits that produce the voice. Reproduce them exactly.

### 7.1 Rhythm and variety
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Long, rolling sentences for exposition; short, punchy sentences for impact. A short sentence after a long one lands hard.
- The sample demonstrates this at every turn:
  - *"A little boy doesn't study his dad in order to become his son — he's already his son. He copies his dad because he's already his son."* (short, stacked for emphasis)
  - *"Likeness flows from belonging."* (one short sentence carrying the weight of an entire paragraph)
  - *"It is a decision before it is ever a feeling."* (same — short, definitive, memorable)
- **Lead paragraphs with the point, then unfold it.** Topic-sentence-first. Never bury the thesis mid-paragraph.

### 7.2 Punctuation and typography
- **Use the em dash** for the turn, the aside, and the punch: *"You can know the doctrine, sing the songs, carry the Bible, and still be a stranger to the very life the gospel produces."*
- **Use italics for emphasis on a single pivotal word** (*because*, *gave*, *enemies*, *agape*) — sparingly, for genuine stress.
- **Bold is used in the manuscript** to mark the load-bearing phrase a listener should not miss: *"**who they are**,"* *"**Can the people around you see the Father in you?**"*
- **Smart quotes and proper typography** throughout (curly quotes, en dashes in references like 5:1–2, em dashes for breaks).
- **No bullet points in the body of the manuscript** — it is continuous spoken prose. (Bullets belong to the listener outline and preaching notes, not the manuscript, except the numbered two-part invitation.)
- **Ellipses** are used sparingly and deliberately — for trailing thought or reflective pause: *"When people who live with you watch you closely — your spouse, your kids, the people in the pew next to you — do they catch a glimpse of the Father?"*

### 7.3 Person and perspective
- **Second person and first-person plural** dominate: "you," "we," "us." The preacher speaks to the congregation directly and includes himself.
- Never use third-person abstraction: not "one might ask" — rather "let me ask you."
- The pastor standing *with* the congregation: *"Here is the uncomfortable question that story forces on all of us this morning..."*

### 7.4 Sentence patterns to reproduce

| Pattern | How It Works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Question → pause → answer** | Ask a question, let it hang, then answer it | *"How am I supposed to imitate God? [...] If Paul had simply said, 'Be like God,' and stopped there, it would be the most hopeless sentence in the Bible."* |
| **State the objection → meet it** | Name what the listener is thinking, then address it | *"Now if you stop and think about that command, it should stagger you."* (then the turn: *"But he doesn't stop there."*) |
| **A → B contrast** | Two parallel statements showing the contrast | *"Obligation asks, 'What's the minimum I have to do?' Love asks, 'What more can I give?'"* |
| **Stacked short declaratives** | 3+ short sentences in a row for rhythm | *"It heals homes. It holds churches together. It melts the hearts of people who think they're beyond reach."* |
| **The expanded definition** | Word study + plain-English unpacking | *"The word 'walk' means to order your daily life — your ordinary, Monday-through-Saturday steps. And the word 'love' is agape — not a warm feeling, not attraction, not 'I love pancakes.'"* |

### 7.5 Paragraph discipline
- **Paragraphs are short to medium** — readable at a glance while preaching, never dense walls of text. In the sample, most paragraphs are 3–6 lines; none exceeds 8 lines.
- Each paragraph has one job. If a paragraph is doing two things, split it.
- Transitions between paragraphs are clean — no filler, no "Now we see that..." — just the next thought.

### 7.6 Repetition with variation
Used deliberately for rhythm in lists and climaxes:
- *"It heals homes. It holds churches together. It melts the hearts of people who think they're beyond reach."*
- *"You can know the doctrine, sing the songs, carry the Bible, and still be a stranger..."*
- The word *beloved* is used 8+ times in Point I alone — never sloppy, always purposeful.

### 7.7 The read-aloud test
Before a manuscript is final, every paragraph must be **read aloud**. If it doesn't sound natural coming off the page, rewrite it. The manuscript is a speaking document, not a reading document.

## 8. Technical / Document Formatting (.docx)

The manuscript is delivered as a **Microsoft Word .docx** built to this exact specification. Values below are verified from the sample "More Than a Name" .docx file.

### 8.1 Page setup
- **Paper:** US Letter — **12240 × 15840 DXA** (8.5" × 11"). Must be set explicitly; the docx engine defaults to A4 and that is wrong.
- **Margins:** **0.5 inch on all sides** — **720 DXA** (457200 EMU) top/right/bottom/left.

### 8.2 Body text
- **Font:** Calibri — the only font used anywhere in the document.
- **Size:** **16 pt** (= **203200 EMU** = **32 half-points** in the docx engine).
- **Line spacing:** ~1.15 (line value ≈ 276 in docx units).
- **Paragraph spacing:** ~160 after each paragraph for readability.
- Body is continuous prose paragraphs (no bullets except the numbered invitation).

### 8.3 Color palette
| Element | Color | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Title text | Navy | `1F497D` |
| Section headers | Navy | `1F497D` |
| Horizontal rules (borders) | Navy | `1F497D` |
| Scripture citation runs | Navy bold | `1F497D` |
| Closing lines | Navy | `1F497D` |
| Byline / secondary text | Muted gray | `555555` |
| Big Idea box fill | Very light blue | `F2F6FB` |
| Body text | Black | (default) |

### 8.4 Title block (top of page 1, centered)

| Element | Format | Sample |
|---|---|---|
| Title | Calibri **26 pt** (330200 EMU, 52 half-pts), **bold**, **navy `1F497D`**, centered | **More Than a Name** |
| Subtitle | Calibri **~15 pt** (190500 EMU, 30 half-pts), *italic*, black, centered | *Learning to Love the Way Our Father Loves* |
| Passage + (ESV) | Calibri **~14 pt** (177800 EMU, 28 half-pts), centered | Ephesians 5:1–2 (ESV) |
| Church • Byline | Calibri **~13 pt** (165100 EMU, 26 half-pts), gray `555555`, centered | Abundant Life Family Worship Center • Pastor Jason |

### 8.5 Big Idea box
- A single paragraph **bordered on all four sides** with navy `1F497D` (border size ~12, border space ~6).
- **Border element order in the XML must be top → left → bottom → right** (schema requirement — the engine may emit them top/bottom/left/right and fail validation; fix the XML order if so).
- Light blue shading fill `F2F6FB` with **`type="clear"`** (the `val`/type attribute is required or validation fails).
- Label **"Big Idea:"** in **bold italic navy**; the thesis text in *italic* black. Both at body size **16 pt** (203200 EMU, 32 half-pts).
- The box is preceded and followed by normal paragraph spacing.

### 8.6 Section headers (Introduction, I/II/III, Conclusion)
- Calibri **20 pt** (254000 EMU, 40 half-pts), **bold**, **navy `1F497D`**.
- **Navy horizontal rule beneath each header** — implemented as a **paragraph bottom border** (size ~8, color `1F497D`, space ~4). **Do not use a table or empty paragraph as a divider** — use a paragraph bottom border on the header paragraph itself.
- Spacing: ~320 before, ~160 after.
- Point headers additionally include the verse reference in parentheses, e.g., *"I. There Is a Person to Imitate (v. 1)"*

### 8.7 Sub-headers ("Application")
- Calibri **17 pt** (215900 EMU, 34 half-pts), **bold**, black.
- Spacing ~200 before, ~100 after.
- No border beneath. No color.

### 8.8 Scripture anchor block
- Indented (~360 DXA left indent).
- Entire quotation in *italic*, **16 pt** (203200 EMU).
- Citation run in **bold navy `1F497D`**.
- Preceded by the spoken cue: *"Listen to the text."*

### 8.9 Invitation / Response
- A **numbered list** (decimal), using the List Paragraph style.
- Indent left 720 / hanging 360.
- Two items only:
  - **Item 1:** "If you are — then go and live like it..." — addressed to those already in Christ.
  - **Item 2:** "If you are not — then hear the better news first..." — addressed to those not yet in Christ.
- Each item opens with a **bold** lead clause (e.g., **"If you are —"** ), then plain text.

### 8.10 Closing lines
- One or two paragraphs.
- **Centered**, **bold italic**, **navy `1F497D`**, **16 pt** (203200 EMU).
- Short, poetic, memorable. They crystallize the entire message in a sentence or two.
- From the sample:
  > *We were never meant to be Christian in name only.*
  > *We were loved into a family — so let us love like the family we belong to.*

### 8.11 Build & validation workflow
- Build using the Node.js **`docx`** package.
- Pattern: construct the `Document` object with sections → `Packer.toBuffer()` → `fs.writeFileSync` to output path.
- **Validate** the resulting .docx file using the docx validation script.
- If validation fails on borders or shading: **unpack the .docx → fix the XML element order / add missing attributes → repack**.
- **Common gotchas** (all encountered and solved on "More Than a Name"):
  - Page size defaults to A4 — set US Letter explicitly in the section properties.
  - Shading needs `type="clear"` (a `val`/type attribute is required, not just the fill color).
  - Paragraph border children must be ordered **top, left, bottom, right** in the XML.
  - Never use unicode bullet characters — use the docx numbering config.
  - Never use a table as a horizontal divider — use a paragraph bottom border.
  - The `spacing` object in docx uses twips (half-points), not points — multiply pt values by 2.
  - Border sizes are 1/8 point units — size 8 = 1 pt, size 12 = 1.5 pt.

### 8.12 File naming
- Descriptive, passage-first, role-tagged. Pattern:
  `Eph_5_1-2_More_Than_a_Name_Manuscript.docx`
  i.e., `<Book_Chapter_Verses>_<Title_Words>_<DeliverableType>.docx`
- Deliverable type suffixes: `Manuscript`, `SermonNotes`, `ListenerOutline`

## 9. Quick Pre-Delivery Checklist

Before handing over a manuscript, confirm every item:

- [ ] **Theological spine:** Grace precedes command — indicatives before imperatives, throughout.
- [ ] **Exposition:** Central idea of the sermon = central idea of the passage.
- [ ] **Translation:** ESV used everywhere; any other translation silently converted.
- [ ] **Big Idea:** Stated in one boxed sentence at the top.
- [ ] **Structure:** Three main points (when the text allows), with parallel/alliterated headers if natural.
- [ ] **Introduction flow:** Opening illustration → central question → context bridge → "Listen to the text" → Scripture → "therefore" takeaway → three points announced.
- [ ] **Bookend:** Opening illustration present; conclusion reaches back to it.
- [ ] **Illustrations:** One strong illustration per point; each immediately tied back to the text with an explicit connective.
- [ ] **Illustration framing:** Any borrowed/fiction story framed honestly; any attributed quote verified.
- [ ] **Point rhythm:** Each point follows state/explain/illustrate/apply movement.
- [ ] **Point III gospel anchor:** Command grounded in Christ's work so it never collapses into moralism.
- [ ] **Application:** Each point ends with concrete, pastoral, this-week Application + a takeaway question + a grace-anchor.
- [ ] **Christ-centered:** At least one point drives to the cross/gospel where the text supports it.
- [ ] **Tone match:** Tone matches the text (weighty where weighty, tender where tender).
- [ ] **Conclusion flow:** Bookend → restate question → what's at stake → decisive question → invitation → closing lines.
- [ ] **Invitation:** Short numbered two-pronged invitation (believer / not-yet-believer), warm and non-coercive.
- [ ] **Closing lines:** One or two centered bold-italic navy lines.
- [ ] **Formatting:** Calibri 16 pt, 0.5" margins, US Letter, navy `1F497D` accents, all verified.
- [ ] **Read-aloud test:** Every paragraph sounds natural when spoken aloud.
- [ ] **Validation:** .docx validated; layout eyeballed; named per convention.
